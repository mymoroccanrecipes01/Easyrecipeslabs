Cheesy Funeral Potatoes: Balancing Moisture and Managing Starch Gelatinization

Lummy Hayes
Published on January 28, 2026
50 min prep
35 min cook
85 min total
8 servings
easy

Learn to make funeral potatoes with a perfectly balanced texture: creamy without being soggy, cheesy without being heavy. This guide covers managing the moisture released by frozen potatoes, achieving proper starch gelatinization, and preventing the common pitfall of ending up with a watery casserole.

Why This Recipe Works

Funeral potatoes succeed because they balance several competing forces in casserole chemistry. The primary challenge is managing moisture. Frozen hash browns contain starch and water. When thawed, they release water as the ice crystals melt. If this water isn't removed, the casserole becomes watery and soupy instead of creamy. This is why the thawing and squeezing step is absolutely critical—it removes the excess water before other ingredients are added, preventing a watery final product.

Once thawed and squeezed, the hash browns are essentially rehydrated shredded potato with starch. When the casserole is heated in the oven, two things happen to this starch. First, it gelatinizes—the starch granules absorb water and swell, creating a thickened texture. Second, the cell walls break down slightly from heat exposure, releasing some starch into the surrounding liquid, which further thickens the sauce. This natural starch release from the potatoes themselves is crucial because it allows the dish to thicken without needing added thickener like cornstarch or flour.

The sour cream and cream of chicken soup form the sauce base. Cream of chicken soup contains starch, fat, and water in an emulsified system. The starch comes from the soup base, the fat comes from cream, and these are held together by the soup's gelatin and emulsifiers. Sour cream adds additional fat and creates a tangy flavor. Together, these two dairy components create a sauce that's creamy but not excessively thick. The ratio of sour cream to soup is important: too much sour cream makes the dish tangy and less binding, while too much soup makes it goopy. This 1:1 ratio is balanced specifically for these competing needs.

Butter serves multiple purposes. It adds richness and contributes to a silky mouthfeel. It also helps distribute flavoring compounds (garlic powder and onion powder are fat-soluble and dissolve readily in melted butter). Additionally, the butter provides fat that doesn't evaporate during baking, maintaining the dish's creamy texture even as the casserole bakes and some water is released and evaporates.

Cheese is crucial for flavor and texture. Sharp cheddar provides more intense flavor than mild, so you can use less cheese and still have strong cheese flavor. Cheese contains casein proteins that unfold slightly when heated above approximately 100°F (38°C), allowing the cheese to melt and flow. However, if overheated above 150°F (65°C) for extended periods, the casein proteins break down further and separate from the fat, causing the cheese to become grainy and oil to leak out. By baking at 350°F (175°C) for approximately 35 minutes, the cheese melts and becomes integrated into the sauce without overheating enough to break down completely.

The cornflake topping adds textural contrast and creates visual appeal through browning. Cornflakes contain carbohydrates that undergo the Maillard reaction when heated above 300°F (149°C). The interaction of the cornflake's proteins and sugars creates brown color and nutty flavor. The crushed cornflakes also provide structural contrast—when you bite into the casserole, you encounter the soft, creamy potatoes below and the crisp, slightly sweet cornflake texture above.

The moisture balance in this casserole is delicate. Too much liquid, and the result is soup. Too little, and it becomes dense and dry. The key is removing water from the thawed hash browns, using the right proportions of sour cream and soup (which contribute moisture controlled by their concentration), and allowing some evaporation during the 30-35 minute baking period. The starch from the potatoes and the soup thickens naturally, so additional thickeners aren't needed. This is why careful attention to the thawing step is essential to final success.

Pro Tips

Thaw and squeeze the frozen hash browns thoroughly—this is the single most important step for preventing a watery casserole. Remove as much water as possible before mixing with other ingredients.
Frozen potatoes contain significant water. If this water isn't removed, it contributes to the casserole becoming watery and soupy rather than creamy. The water released during thawing is pure water with minimal starch or flavor, so removing it doesn't affect the dish negatively—it only improves the final texture by preventing excess liquid.
Use sharp cheddar cheese rather than mild. Sharp cheddar has more intense flavor, so you can use less cheese and still have strong cheese taste. This prevents the dish from becoming too heavy or greasy.
Mild cheddar requires more volume to achieve the same flavor intensity as sharp. Using 2 cups of mild cheese would create a heavier, greasier casserole. Sharp cheddar delivers maximum flavor with minimum cheese volume, resulting in a better balanced final texture.
Don't use cream of mushroom or cream of celery soup as substitutes for cream of chicken soup without testing first. Each soup has different starch content and viscosity, which affects how the casserole thickens.
The proportions in this recipe are calibrated for cream of chicken soup's specific thickness and starch content. Using a different cream soup changes the moisture content and thickening ability of the casserole. You might end up with a mixture that's too thick or too thin.
Ensure sour cream is at room temperature before mixing. Cold sour cream is thick and difficult to blend evenly with other ingredients. Room temperature sour cream mixes smoothly and distributes evenly.
Cold sour cream clumps when you try to mix it, creating pockets of unmixed sour cream rather than even distribution. Room temperature sour cream flows and blends seamlessly. This is a small but important detail that affects texture consistency.
If you're making this the night before serving, assemble it completely (including the cornflake topping), cover it tightly, refrigerate it, and bake the next day. Add 5-10 minutes to the baking time since the casserole will be cold when it enters the oven.
Make-ahead casseroles cook from a cold starting point, so they require slightly more time to heat through completely. The starch gelatinization process is the same, but the longer heating time ensures the entire dish reaches the proper temperature from edge to center.
Use an oven thermometer to verify your oven's actual baking temperature. Many home ovens are 25°F warmer or cooler than indicated, which significantly affects browning time.
If your oven runs hot, the casserole will brown too quickly on top while the interior might not heat through completely. If your oven runs cool, the casserole will take longer to bake than indicated. An inexpensive oven thermometer ($5-10) removes this guesswork.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using frozen hash browns without thawing and squeezing them, which results in a watery, soupy casserole

Why it happens The thawing and squeezing step takes 35-45 minutes, and people want to save time by skipping it. It's not immediately obvious how much difference this step makes until the casserole comes out of the oven watery instead of creamy.

Fix Thaw the hash browns completely and squeeze them firmly in a towel to remove excess water. This is not optional. The water released during thawing is pure water with no starch or flavor, so removing it doesn't harm the dish—it only prevents the casserole from becoming unpleasantly watery.

Overmixing the casserole mixture, which breaks up the hash brown strands and creates a smooth, dense texture instead of a chunky, creamy texture

Why it happens Vigorous mixing seems like it should create a better blended mixture. People mix until the consistency looks very smooth and uniform, not realizing this breaks the hash brown structure.

Fix Mix gently until all ingredients are evenly distributed. Stop when you can no longer see unmixed pockets of sour cream or cheese, but while hash brown strands are still visible. The mixture should look like thick mashed potatoes with visible texture, not smooth purée.

Using cream of mushroom soup or other cream soup varieties instead of cream of chicken soup, which changes the starch content and results in an overly thick or watery casserole

Why it happens People assume all cream soups are interchangeable. They're not—each has different viscosity, starch content, and flavor profile.

Fix Use cream of chicken soup specifically. If you want to experiment with other soups, understand that you may need to adjust the proportions of sour cream or add more liquid to compensate.

Not removing the casserole at the exact right time, resulting in either an underbaked center (too soon) or a watery casserole with overcooked potatoes and broken-down cheese (too late)

Why it happens The window between perfectly cooked and overcooked is approximately 5 minutes. It's difficult to judge the exact moment without experience. The casserole can look done when it's actually still slightly underbaked inside.

Fix Look for actively bubbling edges and a golden-brown top as your primary doneness indicators. Insert a fork into the center and confirm that the mixture shows creamy sauce around the potatoes, not watery liquid. If you see watery liquid, the casserole has released too much water during cooking and is overdone.

Pressing the cornflake topping down firmly into the mixture before baking, which causes it to absorb moisture from the casserole and become soggy instead of crispy

Why it happens People want to ensure the topping stays in place, so they press it down. This seems logical, but it actually prevents the topping from becoming crispy.

Fix Sprinkle the cornflake-cheese mixture loosely on top without pressing down. The cornflakes will toast and crisp during baking while the cheese helps bind them. Loose topping becomes crispy and delicious; pressed topping becomes soggy.

Serving the casserole immediately after removing from the oven, which results in a looser consistency and a very hot cheese mixture

Why it happens The casserole looks done and smells amazing, so people want to serve it immediately. It's hard to wait when the dish is ready.

Fix Let the casserole rest for exactly 5 minutes after removing from the oven. During this time, the residual heat continues cooking the starch, the cornflakes become crispier, and the entire casserole firms up to the ideal serving consistency. Additionally, the cheese cools to a safer eating temperature.

Variations

Bacon and Green Onion Funeral Potatoes

Cook 6-8 slices of bacon until crispy, then crumble it into approximately 3/4 cup (45g) of pieces. Add the cooked bacon and 1/4 cup (20g) of fresh sliced green onions (white and light green parts) to the casserole mixture in step 3, before transferring to the baking dish. The bacon adds salty, smoky flavor and textural crunch. The green onions add brightness and a subtle onion flavor that complements the garlic powder already in the dish. Use the same baking time and temperature.

Note: Cooked bacon adds fat, which contributes to richness, but the moisture content is minimal, so it doesn't affect the casserole's overall liquid balance. Green onions are mostly water, but the amount added (1/4 cup) is too small to significantly change the moisture balance. They will wilt into the casserole during baking but add flavor throughout.

Broccoli and Cheese Funeral Potatoes

Add 2 cups (200g) of fresh broccoli florets, finely chopped into small pieces (approximately 1/2-inch / 1cm pieces), to the mixture in step 3. The broccoli adds vegetables and nutritional value to the casserole. Use the same other ingredients and baking time. The broccoli will soften during baking and become tender without becoming mushy.

Note: Fresh broccoli contains moisture, which is released during heating. This adds approximately 1/4 to 1/3 cup (60-80ml) of water to the casserole. If you add broccoli, you may want to reduce the sour cream by 2-3 tablespoons (30-45ml) to compensate for this additional moisture, otherwise the casserole may become slightly looser than desired. Alternatively, make sure to squeeze the thawed hash browns extra thoroughly.

Vegetarian Funeral Potatoes

Substitute the cream of chicken soup with cream of mushroom soup or cream of celery soup (same amount, 1 can undiluted). This maintains the casserole's structure while making it vegetarian. The flavor will be slightly different—cream of mushroom is earthier, cream of celery is more subtle—but both work adequately. All other ingredients remain the same, including the baking time and temperature.

Note: Different cream soups have slightly different starch and viscosity levels. Cream of mushroom is typically slightly thicker than cream of chicken, so the casserole might be marginally firmer. Cream of celery is slightly thinner. If you find the final consistency unsatisfactory, adjust in future batches by adding 2-3 tablespoons of sour cream or soup accordingly.

Gluten-Free Funeral Potatoes

Replace the crushed cornflakes topping with crushed gluten-free cornflakes (such as Fritos brand gluten-free or Chex cereal). Verify that the cream of chicken soup is gluten-free, as some commercial varieties contain gluten thickeners or additives. All other ingredients in the casserole are naturally gluten-free. The baking time and temperature remain the same.

Note: Gluten-free cereal products sometimes toast differently than regular cornflakes because they often have different fat and protein compositions. Watch the casserole closely during baking to ensure the topping browns appropriately. The flavor may be slightly different depending on the gluten-free product chosen, but the casserole should still turn out well.

Ham and Cheese Funeral Potatoes

Add 1.5 cups (225g) of diced cooked ham (approximately 1/2-inch / 1cm pieces) to the mixture in step 3. Reduce the cream of chicken soup to 3/4 can (approximately 225ml) because ham contributes saltiness and flavor. Alternatively, use the full can of soup and reduce the added salt from 1/2 teaspoon to 1/4 teaspoon. The ham transforms this into a heartier casserole that works as a main dish rather than a side. Bake using the same time and temperature.

Note: Cooked ham is predominantly protein and fat with minimal moisture content. It doesn't significantly alter the casserole's liquid balance but adds substantial flavor. However, ham is salty, so be cautious about total salt content to avoid over-salting the dish. Taste before serving and adjust if needed.

Three-Cheese Funeral Potatoes

Use 1.5 cups (170g) sharp cheddar cheese and replace 0.5 cup (55g) of the topping cheddar with equal parts gruyere and Parmesan. Gruyere adds a slightly nutty, complex flavor. Parmesan adds salty, umami notes and helps the topping brown more deeply due to its higher melting point. The ratio becomes approximately 1.5 cups cheddar in the mixture, 0.25 cup gruyere, and 0.25 cup Parmesan in the topping. The baking time remains the same.

Note: Different cheeses have different melting temperatures. Gruyere melts at a slightly higher temperature than cheddar, and Parmesan is more resistant to melting. This blend creates a topping that browns more deeply and crisps more effectively than all-cheddar. The mixed cheeses also create a more complex flavor profile than single-cheese versions.

Storage & Reheating

Refrigerator

Store completely cooled casserole in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 3 days. The starch in the potatoes and the sauce will firm up significantly as the casserole cools, which is normal. Cold casserole is actually easier to serve because it holds its shape. Do not freeze this casserole if possible, as the repeated thawing and freezing cycle further breaks down the potato structure and can create a mushy texture upon reheating.

Freezer

Although not ideal, you can freeze cooled casserole in an airtight freezer-safe container for up to 2 months if necessary. The potato structure will be slightly softer upon reheating than a never-frozen casserole. Thaw completely in the refrigerator (12-24 hours) before reheating. Do not reheat from frozen as this will create uneven texture.

Reheating

Reheat in the oven at 325°F (163°C) for 25-30 minutes, covered loosely with aluminum foil. The lower temperature prevents excessive browning of the top while the interior heats through. If you want the top to re-crisp (the cornflakes will have softened during refrigerated storage), remove the foil during the final 5 minutes of reheating. Alternatively, reheat individual portions in the microwave on 50% power for 3-4 minutes, stirring halfway through. The stovetop reheating method is not recommended as it can cause uneven heating and may separate the sauce from the potatoes.

Make Ahead

Assemble the casserole completely (including the cornflake topping) in the morning, cover it tightly with plastic wrap, and refrigerate until evening or the next day. Bake it directly from the refrigerator, adding 10-15 minutes to the baking time since the casserole starts cold. This approach is ideal for entertaining, as you can prepare the dish the day before and simply pop it in the oven when your guests arrive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my funeral potatoes casserole watery and soupy instead of creamy?
The most common cause is not thoroughly thawing and squeezing the frozen hash browns before mixing. Frozen potatoes release significant water when thawed. If this water isn't removed, the casserole becomes watery. Additionally, baking longer than 35 minutes causes additional water to release from the potatoes and the sauce to break down. Remove the casserole when the edges are actively bubbling and the top is golden brown. If the casserole is already watery after cooking, you can strain off excess liquid through a fine-mesh strainer and serve with a slotted spoon.
Can I make funeral potatoes without using cream of chicken soup?
You can substitute with cream of mushroom or cream of celery soup, though the flavor will be different. Alternatively, you can make a simple sauce by whisking 1/2 cup (120ml) of chicken or vegetable broth with 2 tablespoons (15g) of all-purpose flour and adding it along with the sour cream and butter. The flour provides thickening that the canned soup would normally provide. However, testing is recommended as the texture and flavor will differ from the original recipe.
How do I prevent the cornflake topping from becoming soggy?
Do not press the topping down before baking. Sprinkle the cornflake-cheese mixture loosely on the surface. The cornflakes will crisp and brown during the 30-35 minute baking time if left loose. Additionally, mixing the cornflakes with shredded cheese helps protect them from absorbing moisture. If the topping seems soft after cooking, you can place it back in a 400°F (200°C) oven for an additional 2-3 minutes to recrisp it, though this risks slightly overdrying the casserole underneath.
Can I make funeral potatoes ahead and freeze them?
You can freeze assembled (unbaked) casserole for up to 1 month by covering it tightly and freezing directly. Bake it from the frozen state, adding 15-20 minutes to the baking time. Alternatively, bake the casserole completely, cool it, and freeze for up to 2 months. Thaw in the refrigerator and reheat at 325°F (163°C) for 25-30 minutes. Freezing and thawing does soften the potato texture slightly, but the casserole is still acceptable.
Is funeral potatoes a side dish or a main course?
Traditionally, funeral potatoes are a side dish served at gatherings alongside other mains. However, if you add bacon, ham, or extra protein, it can work as a light main course. The original recipe with 2 pounds of potatoes and 2 cups of cheese makes approximately 8 side dish servings or 4-6 main course servings depending on appetite and what else is served.
Why does the recipe call for crushing the cornflakes instead of using whole flakes?
Crushed cornflakes create a more even distribution across the casserole top and toast more uniformly. Whole cornflakes would be too large and might not toast evenly. Additionally, crushed cornflakes create a more manageable topping that stays in place during serving rather than individual flakes that might shift around the casserole.

Ingredients

8 servings in a 9x13 inch baking dish

  • 2 pounds (900g) frozen hash browns (shredded style, not cubed), thawed and squeezed dry
  • 1 cup (240ml) sour cream, room temperature
  • 1 can (10.5 oz / 298g) cream of chicken soup, undiluted
  • 2 cups (225g) shredded sharp cheddar cheese, divided (1.5 cups for mixing, 0.5 cup for topping)
  • 1/2 cup (115g) unsalted butter, melted
  • 1/2 cup (30g) crushed cornflakes cereal
  • 1/2 teaspoon (1g) garlic powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon (1g) onion powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon (3g) fine sea salt
  • 1/4 teaspoon (0.5g) black pepper
  • Optional additions: 6 slices cooked bacon (45g), crumbled; 1/4 cup (20g) sliced green onions (add before baking)

Instructions

Step 1
Remove the frozen hash browns from the freezer and place them in a strainer or colander. Let them thaw at room temperature for approximately 30-40 minutes until completely thawed. You'll see water collecting at the bottom of the strainer. Once thawed, wrap the hash browns in a clean kitchen towel or several paper towels and squeeze firmly to remove as much water as possible. Squeeze in several directions to ensure thorough water removal. You should release approximately 1/4 to 1/3 cup (60-80ml) of water. This squeezing step is absolutely critical and cannot be skipped. Removing this water prevents the casserole from becoming watery and soupy. The hash browns will appear visibly drier and will stick together slightly from the removed starch, which is exactly right.
⏱ Time: 30-40 minutes thawing plus 5 minutes squeezing
✓ Doneness: Hash browns are completely thawed, no ice visible. After squeezing, the towel is noticeably wet and the hash browns are noticeably drier than when first placed in the towel.
Step 2
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) for at least 15 minutes. Use an oven thermometer to verify the actual temperature, as many home ovens run hotter or cooler than indicated. While the oven preheats, prepare a 9x13 inch (23x33cm) baking dish by lightly greasing it with butter or non-stick cooking spray. Set aside.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Oven has reached 350°F (175°C) and stabilized at that temperature. Baking dish is prepared and ready to receive the mixture.
Step 3
In a large mixing bowl, combine the thawed and squeezed hash browns, sour cream (ensure it's at room temperature to mix smoothly), cream of chicken soup (use the soup undiluted directly from the can), melted butter, 1.5 cups (170g) of the shredded cheddar cheese (reserve the remaining 0.5 cup for topping), garlic powder, onion powder, salt, and pepper. Mix the ingredients together using a sturdy spoon or your hands (hands are actually more effective here). Stir until the hash browns are evenly coated with the creamy mixture. Do not overmix—you're aiming for uniform distribution, but aggressive mixing will break up the hash brown strands and create a paste rather than a chunky casserole. The mixture should look like creamy mashed potatoes with visible shreds of potato throughout, not smooth and uniform like mashed potatoes. If using optional bacon or green onions, add them now and fold in gently.
⏱ Time: 5-7 minutes
✓ Doneness: All ingredients are evenly distributed. Hash brown strands are visible and not broken into tiny pieces. The mixture is creamy but maintains some texture.
Step 4
Transfer the entire mixture to the prepared 9x13 inch baking dish. Use a spatula to spread it into an even layer, pressing slightly to compact it gently. The mixture should spread to the edges of the baking dish and be approximately 2-2.5 inches (5-6cm) thick when spread evenly.
⏱ Time: 2-3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture is evenly spread in the baking dish with no large gaps. The surface is relatively level with only slight irregularities.
Step 5
In a small bowl, combine the crushed cornflakes with the remaining 0.5 cup (55g) of shredded cheddar cheese. Stir to distribute the cheese evenly throughout the cornflakes. This topping provides the textural contrast and prevents the cornflakes from absorbing moisture from the casserole below. The cheese helps bind the cornflakes together slightly and prevents them from becoming soggy during baking. Sprinkle this mixture evenly across the top of the casserole. Don't press it down—let it sit loosely on the surface so it can brown and crisp during baking.
⏱ Time: 2-3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cornflake and cheese mixture is evenly distributed across the top with no bare spots. Topping is loose and fluffy, not packed down.
Step 6
Place the baking dish in the preheated 350°F (175°C) oven. Bake for 30-35 minutes. The casserole is done when the edges are visibly bubbling (you'll see sauce bubbling up around the edges where it contacts the baking dish), the top is golden brown, and the cornflake topping is crispy and shows browning. At the 25-minute mark, check the casserole to see if browning is occurring evenly. If the top is browning faster than you'd like, you can place a piece of aluminum foil loosely over the top to slow browning while the interior finishes cooking. The cheese inside should be completely melted, and the potato mixture should be heated throughout. The texture inside, when you insert a fork, should show a creamy sauce surrounding the potato shreds, not a watery mixture.
⏱ Time: 30-35 minutes
✓ Doneness: Edges are actively bubbling with sauce visible around the perimeter. Top is golden brown with crispy cornflakes. A fork inserted into the center shows creamy, cohesive mixture with no watery liquid surrounding the potatoes.
Step 7
Remove the casserole from the oven and let it rest undisturbed for exactly 5 minutes before serving. During this resting period, the casserole continues to cook slightly from residual heat, and the starch in the potatoes continues to absorb liquid, creating a firmer texture. This resting period is essential—if you serve immediately, the casserole may appear slightly looser and the cornflake topping won't have set as crisply. Additionally, the cheese will still be extremely hot (likely above 160°F / 71°C), so resting allows it to cool slightly to a safer serving temperature. After 5 minutes, the casserole is ready to serve.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes resting
✓ Doneness: Casserole has stopped actively bubbling. Top is fully set and crispy. Interior is at a safe serving temperature (approximately 140-150°F / 60-65°C).

Final Thoughts

Funeral potatoes teach fundamental casserole construction: balancing moisture, managing starch gelatinization, properly emulsifying dairy and fat, and achieving textural contrast through toppings. The technical principles behind this simple dish—removing water from frozen vegetables, understanding how potato starch thickens sauce, knowing when cheese starts to break down if overheated—apply to countless other casserole recipes. Once you understand why each step matters, you can troubleshoot problems with confidence and adapt the recipe based on what you're serving and who you're serving it to.
